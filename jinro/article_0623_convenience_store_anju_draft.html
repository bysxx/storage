The TikTok/Reels Convenience-Store Anju Hacks Koreans Actually Make

A trend check on what's actually viral on Korean convenience-store FoodTok right now, from cold Buldak noodles to corn cheese, with the JINRO pour that fits each one.

If it looks like someone filmed it over a microwave, it probably belongs here.

So here is the trend check. The viral anju in Korea right now is not a new product on the shelf. It is the modisumer move (modify plus consumer): buy a few ordinary items, combine them fast, and get a bowl that looks good enough for TikTok, Reels, or Shorts. The behavior is big enough to move what stores stock. The "ttungba latte," just pouch coffee and banana milk poured over a cup of store ice, pushed cup-ice sales at one major chain up more than 50% year over year this past month. The drinks are their own thing. What follows is the spicy, salty side that is actually trending as anju right now.

Buldak naengmyeon is the summer feed pick

Buldak naengmyeon is simple enough to spread: spicy Buldak noodles boiled and chilled in ice water, then dropped into a cold naengmyeon broth whisked with the Buldak sauce, a splash of vinegar, and a little sugar. Ice on top, cucumber and a halved boiled egg if you want the full look. It blew up across Korean feeds this May as the weather warmed, because red noodles over ice is peak warm-season content.

The cold heat wants something bright, so JINRO Grapefruit or JINRO Plum fits without weighing the bowl down.

The hot-broth ramen upgrade is the rainy-day move

Most Korean convenience stores have an in-store hot-water tap or a little induction burner by the counter, which is the whole reason this one works. Take a basic cup or pot ramen and level it up on the spot: melt a slice of cheese into the broth, slide in a soft-boiled egg, and stir a spoon of spicy sauce through it so a plain bowl turns into a richer, savory one. It is the quiet, no-recipe hack people reach for on a cold or rainy night.

A hot, savory bowl like this pairs cleanly with Chamisul Fresh, which stays light against the broth.

Mark Meal is still the blueprint

Mark Meal is the older legend that still explains the whole category: cup tteokbokki, spaghetti cup ramen, a frank sausage, and mozzarella string cheese, microwaved together for about a minute until it becomes one spicy, messy rabokki-style bowl.

The name has a story. It is named after GOT7's Mark Tuan: a fan won a recipe contest run by the cup-tteokbokki maker and dedicated the dish to him so more people would learn his name. It went viral in 2019 and still shows up in 2026 convenience-store guides, because the formula is exactly what short-form food wants: clear ingredients, one microwave moment, and a strong cheese pull.

For a salty, spicy, rice-cake-heavy bowl like this, Chamisul Original is the clean classic pour.

Buldak, triangle kimbap, and cheese turns into a spicy rice bowl

Search Korean convenience-store food on TikTok and the pattern shows up fast: Buldak noodles, a triangle kimbap, cheese, and sometimes a hot-bar sausage. The move is to mash the triangle kimbap into the leftover Buldak sauce so it eats like a spicy fried-rice bowl, then layer a slice of cheese on top. Plenty of the clips going around are foreign visitors filming exactly this at the counter, which is how a simple remix travels.

The bowl is salty, spicy, and rice-heavy, so Chamisul Original holds up next to it.

Triangle kimbap ramen-juk is the low-budget late-night move

Another feed-friendly hack is ramen-juk: cook a cup ramen, drop in a triangle kimbap (tuna-mayo is a common pick), break it up, and microwave it briefly so the rice thickens the broth into a porridge. An egg stirred through makes it richer.

It is not flashy in a restaurant way. It is warm, salty, cheap, and built for a convenience-store table after midnight, which is exactly when a cold Chamisul tends to be on it.

Buldak corn cheese is the party-table version

Corn cheese already belongs at a Korean drinking table. Add Buldak sauce or noodles, broil it until the top goes golden, and it becomes short-form food: red sauce, yellow corn, and a long cheese pull when you lift the spoon for the video payoff.

Chamisul Fresh keeps the corn-and-cheese bowl from feeling too heavy.

The filter is the hack, not the brand

The strongest convenience-store anju ideas are not the ones with the biggest launch campaign. They are the ones people can recreate with a basket, a microwave, and a phone camera, which is why the same modisumer logic keeps spinning off classics like chapaguri and rabokki.
