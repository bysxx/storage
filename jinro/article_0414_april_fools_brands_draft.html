KFC Made Chicken Toothpaste. Duolingo Launched a Cruise. So What Did JINRO Do for April Fools'?

Every year on April 1st, brands compete to out-prank each other — and some of these "jokes" actually become real products. Here's our roundup of the wildest campaigns, what JINRO did (or didn't do), and why that might actually be the most interesting thing on this list.

April Fools' Day has quietly become one of the biggest marketing moments of the year. No media buys. No influencer contracts. Just one rule: make something so absurd that people can't tell if it's real — and watch the internet do the rest.

The best brand pranks generate millions of impressions, trending hashtags, and the kind of earned media that money can't buy. And when they're done really well? The fake product becomes a real one.

We went through the best campaigns from the past few years and picked the ones that actually moved the needle. Here's what stood out.

The 2025 Class: When Brands Went Full Chaos

Last year's April Fools' campaigns raised the bar significantly. Here are the ones that dominated social feeds.

Duolingo × Carnival Cruise Line: The Duolingo World Cruise — A five-year global voyage with language immersion, cultural experiences, and — according to the announcement — "an unreasonable amount of shrimp." Miss a lesson on the ship? The Duolingo owl threatened to deny you "eternal peace." The campaign offered real prizes including a free month of Super Duolingo and cruise discounts, blurring the line between prank and promotion.

KFC × Hismile: Fried Chicken Toothpaste — Here's the thing — this one was real. KFC and oral care brand Hismile actually produced and sold fried chicken-flavored toothpaste for $13. Cardi B promoted it. People bought it. It sold out. When your April Fools' prank is a real product that actually ships, you've won the game.

Sour Patch Kids: "We're Removing the Sour" — Sour Patch Kids announced they were permanently removing the sour coating from their candy, leaving only the sweet gummy underneath. Fans panicked. Social media erupted. Engagement through the roof. It was, of course, a joke — but for a few terrifying hours, sour candy lovers genuinely feared for their favorite snack.

Dunkin': One Million Free Coffees — While everyone else was lying, Dunkin' did the opposite. They offered one million free coffees to Rewards members with the code "ThisIsNotAJoke." No prank. No fake product. Just a real deal on the one day nobody trusts anything. The irony was the campaign.

Olipop × Hidden Valley Ranch: Ranch-Flavored Soda — Olipop announced a "Ranch Lovers Pack" with flavors including Garlic Ranch soda. Creators received real Olipop cans in Hidden Valley branding to post about. What started as a joke generated so much demand that Olipop reportedly explored actually producing a limited run.

Welch's Fruit Snacks: $19 Single Strawberry Gummy — A single gourmet strawberry fruit snack, priced at $19. A direct parody of Erewhon's infamous $19 smoothies and the luxury wellness industrial complex. Simple, funny, and perfectly timed.

Crunch Bar: The Silent Crunch — Crunch announced a new version made with cooked rice instead of crispy puffed rice — eliminating the signature crunch sound entirely. A candy bar defined by its sound, now silent. Brilliantly absurd, and it perfectly demonstrated how central that one characteristic is to the brand's identity.

2024 Highlights: The Year Fake Products Almost Became Real

2024 was the year brands realized that the best April Fools' pranks are the ones people actually want to buy.

Smoothie King: $41.24 "Air-Wan" Smoothie — Named after CEO Wan Kim, this absurdly priced smoothie was a direct shot at Erewhon's celebrity smoothie culture. The "luxurious primary ingredient"? Air. At $41.24, it cost more than most people's lunch for a week. A perfect satire of wellness culture excess.

Krispy Kreme: "You Bring It, We Glaze It" — Krispy Kreme announced they'd glaze anything you bring in — cheeseburgers, pizza, produce, you name it. Photos of glazed burgers went viral. The prank was fake, but the real deal was not: they offered a dozen Original Glazed donuts for $4.01 (April 1st, get it?).

7-Eleven × Miracle Seltzer: Hot Dog Sparkling Water — Big Bite Hot Dog flavored sparkling water — complete with ketchup and mustard notes. 7-Eleven called it a joke, then admitted they'd made a limited supply because they "couldn't help themselves." When the line between prank and product disappears, that's peak April Fools' marketing.

Sweetgreen: After Salad Kit — Miso Ginger Toothpaste. Lime Cilantro Dental Floss. The "After Salad Kit" for post-lunch freshness. Fans said they'd genuinely buy it, which tells you everything about Sweetgreen's brand loyalty.

The Hall of Fame: April Fools' Campaigns That Changed Marketing

Some pranks transcend the moment. These are the ones that marketing textbooks still reference.

Taco Bell (1996): "We Bought the Liberty Bell" — Taco Bell took out full-page ads in The New York Times, The Washington Post, and USA Today announcing they had purchased the Liberty Bell and were renaming it the "Taco Liberty Bell." Thousands called the National Park Service to complain. The White House had to comment. The stunt generated an estimated $22 million in free publicity and boosted sales by $500,000 in a single day. Thirty years later, it's still the gold standard.

Google (2004): Gmail Launch — Google launched Gmail on April 1st with 1GB of free storage — at a time when competitors offered 2–4MB. The entire internet assumed it was a prank. It wasn't. Gmail went on to become the world's most popular email service with over 1.8 billion users. Sometimes the best April Fools' move is being completely serious on the day no one believes anything.

Burger King (2018): Chocolate Whopper — A Whopper with a chocolate cake bun, chocolate patty, raspberry syrup "ketchup," white chocolate "onion rings," and candied lettuce. The product video was so well-produced that many viewers couldn't tell it was fake. The key to a great food prank: make it look delicious even when it shouldn't be.

IKEA (2025): The Straight-Line Store — IKEA announced a new store concept: a two-kilometer straight line with no maze, no shortcuts, and no Swedish meatball break until the very end. Anyone who's ever been lost in IKEA felt this one deeply. It was shared millions of times because it addressed a universal frustration that IKEA has spent decades pretending doesn't exist.

The Formula: Why Some April Fools' Campaigns Work and Others Don't

After looking at dozens of campaigns, the pattern is clear. The ones that break through share a few traits:

  • The "Is It Real?" Zone — The best pranks sit exactly on the boundary between plausible and absurd. Too obvious, and nobody engages. Too realistic, and you end up like Volkswagen getting investigated by the SEC.
  • Production quality matters — High-quality mockups, videos, and branded visuals increase shareability by 64% compared to text-only announcements. If your fake product looks like a real product, people treat it like one.
  • Self-awareness wins — Duolingo leaning into the "threatening owl" meme. IKEA acknowledging their maze problem. Crunch questioning their own core product attribute. Brands that can laugh at themselves always win April 1st.
  • The prank-to-product pipeline — The ultimate move is when demand is so high that the joke becomes real. Olipop, 7-Eleven, and KFC all turned fake products into actual sales. That's not just marketing — it's product development disguised as comedy.
  • Omnichannel execution — Campaigns that run across TikTok, Instagram, X, and sometimes real-world touchpoints see 3–5x more engagement than single-platform pranks.

So... What Did JINRO Do?

After scrolling through all these global campaigns, we had to ask: has JINRO — the world's best-selling spirit brand — ever played the April Fools' game?

Turns out, they have. And they might have done it better than anyone on this list.

The "One Drop Glass" — The Giant Soju Glass (2018)

It started in 2018. On April 1st, JINRO introduced the "Hanbangul-jan" (한방울잔) — literally the "One Drop Glass." The joke? It was a soju glass the size of your palm that could hold an entire 360ml bottle of Chamisul in a single pour. One bottle, one glass, one pour, one shot.

It was supposed to be a lighthearted April Fools' gag. But drinkers across Korea went wild. Demand was so overwhelming that JINRO actually produced 4,000 limited-edition sets and sold them on Auction — they sold out instantly and the servers crashed within a minute. A joke glass became one of the most sought-after pieces of JINRO merchandise ever made.

Image: The "One Drop Glass" (한방울잔) — a palm-sized soju glass that holds an entire bottle. Started as an April Fools' joke. Sold out in under a minute.
The "19-Rated Toothpaste" (2019)

In 2019, JINRO teamed up with Korean toothpaste brand LG Perioe to create a product called the "19-Rated Toothpaste" — a pumping-type toothpaste bottle designed to look exactly like a Chamisul Fresh soju bottle. Same green tint. Same label design. Same everything — except it was filled with regular bamboo salt toothpaste instead of soju.

The tagline? "#MadeWhileDrunk." The concept was that brushing your teeth after a night out would feel like you never left the drinking table. It was posted on JINRO's official social media on April 1st — and thousands of comments poured in begging them to actually produce it.

Image: JINRO × LG Perioe "19-Rated Toothpaste" (2019) — a toothpaste bottle that looks exactly like Chamisul Fresh. Thousands demanded it become a real product.

Sound familiar? KFC did the exact same playbook six years later with their chicken toothpaste — except JINRO did it first. And while KFC actually produced theirs, the fact that JINRO's version was fake made it arguably more on-brand for April Fools'.

"Hack Isher Eisul" — The April Fools' Prank That Became Real (2025)

But here's where JINRO takes it to the next level.

On April 1st, 2025, JINRO dropped a digital teaser for "Hack Isher Eisul" (핵아이셔에이슬) — a collaboration with Orion's iconic Hack Isher sour candy brand. The concept? An "ultra-extreme sour" soju with a sourness level cranked to maximum. The JINRO Toad mascot appeared winking for the first time ever on the label, squinting from the intensity.

Everyone assumed it was an April Fools' joke.

It wasn't.

Image: "Hack Isher Eisul" — JINRO × Orion's extreme sour soju. Announced on April 1st. Everyone thought it was a joke. It was real. It sold out.

The teaser went viral across Korean social media and online communities. Fans of the original Isher Eisul soju and extreme-flavor seekers flooded the comments. By April 18th, the product hit shelves nationwide — 12% ABV, zero sugar, with a sourness level that made the original version taste mild. Promotional events included sour candy giveaways at university areas in Hongdae and Konkuk, plus an online "sour challenge" campaign.

JINRO pulled off what every brand on this list dreams of: they announced a product on April 1st that was so absurd people assumed it was fake — and then actually released it. The Google Gmail strategy, executed perfectly.
