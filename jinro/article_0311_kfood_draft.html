Bomdong Bibimbap — An 18-Year-Old Clip Just Changed Everything

'Bomdong' is Korean spring cabbage—sweeter and more tender than regular napa, only available in late winter. When a 2008 clip of comedian Kang Ho-dong eating 'Bomdong' bibimbap on KBS resurfaced in February 2026, it hit 6.3 million views overnight—and 'Bomdong' sold out at markets across the country.

The recipe is surprisingly simple: tear fresh 'Bomdong' leaves by hand, lay them over a bowl of warm rice, add gochujang, sesame oil, and a fried egg on top. That's it. The crisp, slightly sweet leaves do most of the work—no elaborate prep needed.

What to pour: JINRO is Back. 'Bomdong's' delicate sweetness calls for something equally clean and understated—JINRO is Back's smooth, no-frills character lets the seasonal flavors do the talking. Pour it cold, keep it simple.

Frozen Gummies — The Trend That Came Back Around

Frozen gummies aren't new—you've probably seen them before. But in Korea right now, the trend just came back with a specific twist. Sour gummies are the flavor of choice, and the preferred shape is thin and long rather than thick and chunky—it's all about that crisp, crackling sound when you bite in. The trend has pushed convenience store gummy sales up by over 65% across Korea. This is Korea's "modisumer" (modify + consumer) culture in action: take something that already exists, remix it into something entirely new, film it, share it, repeat.

Some people soak their gummies in cider before freezing for an extra fizzy crunch. If you want to try it yourself, here's our suggestion: swap the cider for a splash of JINRO Lemon Soju. The citrus kick pairs perfectly with sour gummies, and it fills in that missing 2% you didn't know you needed.

Buldak × Seaweed Soup — The Mashup No One Expected

First, a bit of context: 'Miyeok-guk' (seaweed soup) is a staple in Korean households. It's considered deeply nourishing—traditionally eaten by new mothers for recovery and served on birthdays as a celebration dish. It's mild, savory, and comforting. Not exactly the kind of thing you'd pair with Samyang's notoriously spicy Buldak ramen.

But that's exactly what happened. A mukbang video of someone cooking Buldak noodles straight into miyeok-guk broth went viral—and the reactions were unanimous: everyone was skeptical at first, but after one bite, completely sold. The combination blew up, with convenience stores across Korea reporting Buldak sales up 116% and seaweed soup up 47.5% as people rushed to try it for themselves.

If you've ever grabbed Buldak at a Korean convenience store while traveling (and let's be honest, most of you have), here's your next level-up: look for the ready-made miyeok-guk packets on the shelf and cook them together. And since no Korean soup meal is complete without soju, pour yourself a glass of Chamisul Fresh—it's the soju Koreans reach for with soupy, brothy dishes. Clean, smooth, and the perfect reset between spicy bites.

Halmennial Desserts — Grandma's Recipes Are the Main Character Now

Meet the "Halmennial" wave—halmae (Korean for grandma) meets millennial. Traditional Korean sweets are having a full-blown moment. Yakgwa (deep-fried honey cookies) are showing up inside croissants and on top of ice cream. Gaeseong juak (rice flour donuts glazed in cinnamon syrup) are selling out at Seoul's trendiest cafés. The common thread? Simple ingredients, nothing artificial, and that addictive kkudeok (soft, chewy) texture everyone keeps coming back for.

What to pour: JINRO Plum Soju. Traditional Korean sweets already lean into honey and sesame—JINRO Plum Soju's gentle, mellow sweetness fits right into that flavor family without competing. Think of it as the dessert soju that was made for this moment.

Gil-gamja — The Viral Potato Snack You Can Make at Home

Gil-gamja (literally "street potato") is one of Korea's hottest street food trends right now. Originally from Gangneung's Jungang Market, this snack uses only Gangwon-do potatoes—no flour at all. The potatoes are slow-cooked and mashed with potato starch to create a dense, chewy dough, then deep-fried until impossibly crispy on the outside and stretchy-soft on the inside. The original shop's motto says it all: "Extra crispy, extra chewy."

The best part? You can make it at home—and that's exactly what's trending right now. All you need is potatoes, potato starch, water, and salt. Boil and mash the potatoes, mix in the starch until the dough gets thick and stretchy, then deep-fry. (For a full walkthrough, search "길감자 만들기" on YouTube—there are dozens of great tutorials.)

It's a universally crowd-pleasing potato dish with zero controversy. Make a batch, pour yourself a glass of Chamisul Original—the classic Korean soju that pairs perfectly with hot, salty, crispy comfort food—and enjoy.
