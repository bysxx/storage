Korea's Viral Food Scene Just Changed—Here's What Everyone's Actually Eating Now

The tanghulu lines are thinning. The bakery queues for dujjonku might be getting shorter—because the obsession has moved straight to the bar. Seoul's food scene has already evolved, and the new wave is nothing like what came before.

You think you know Korean food? Think again. While the rest of the world was still catching up on dujjonku and tanghulu, Korea's food scene quietly evolved into something bigger. From a resurfaced 2008 mukbang clip that broke the internet to TikTok's latest frozen jelly ASMR obsession—here's what's actually on everyone's plate in Seoul right now, and what to pour while you eat.

No. 1 Trending

Bomdong Bibimbap — The 18-Year-Old Clip That Broke the Internet

Korean comedian Kang Ho-dong ate a bowl of bomdong bibimbap on KBS back in 2008. Fast forward to February 2026—that clip resurfaced and hit 6.3 million views. Overnight, bomdong went from a niche spring vegetable to Korea's most-searched ingredient. Google Trends jumped from 15 to 100. Online mentions exploded 540%. Wholesale prices surged 78% as demand outpaced supply.

It's the face of Gen Z's "Jecheol Core" movement—eating what's seasonal, local, and affordable. Cheap, healthy, beautiful enough to take over your feed, and gone by April.

Rising Fast

Frozen Jelly — The ASMR Snack That's Everywhere

Freeze a bag of gummy candy for four hours. What you get crunches like ice but melts like candy—and the sound is genuinely mind-bending. Jelly Eol-meok clips racked up 2M+ views on Instagram. GS25 jelly sales jumped 65%. It's Korea's "modisumer" culture at work: buy a product, hack it into something new, film it, post it.

Modisumer Hit

Buldak Miyeoktang — The Remix That Broke Sales Records

Samyang's Buldak ramen + beef seaweed soup. One YouTube mukbang hit 1.6M views, and CU reported Buldak sales up 116% with miyeok-guk up 47.5%. The seaweed soup tames the fire just enough, while the spicy broth upgrades the mild soup. Two things that shouldn't work together, but absolutely do.

· · ·

Still Going Strong

Halmaenial Desserts — Grandma Snacks, Gen Z Approved

"Halmaenial"—a mashup of halmae (grandma) and millennial—turned traditional Korean sweets into Seoul's hottest dessert wave. Yakgwa honey pastries are stuffed in croissants and stacked on ice cream. Gaeseong juak rice donuts are selling out at trendy cafés. The appeal? Simple ingredients, zero artificial anything, and that signature kkudeok chewy texture Gen Z can't get enough of.

Honorable Mentions

Gil-gamja — The OG That Never Misses

Some things don't need a viral moment to stay relevant. Gil-gamja—the gluten-free potato-tapioca fritter born in Gangneung's Jungang Market—still commands 2-hour lines in 2026. Crispy shell, impossibly chewy center, served piping hot. While every other street food trend burns bright and fades, this one just keeps going.
