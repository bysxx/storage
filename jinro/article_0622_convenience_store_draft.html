What Koreans Are Actually Eating at Convenience Stores in 2026

Korean convenience stores have become a travel destination of their own. But what are locals actually picking up, mixing, heating, and eating right now?

Korea's best convenience-store anju right now is not a new product drop. It is the modisumer (modify-plus-consumer) food hack going viral on TikTok, Reels, and Shorts: ramen-juk, jjajang Buldak, cheesy chips, cup-noodle egg steam, and Buldak naengmyeon.

Here is how each one comes together at the counter — and the JINRO pour that fits it.

Start with ramen-juk, the triangle-kimbap upgrade

The most basic move — triangle kimbap with cup ramen — earns a real name once you take it one step further: ramen-juk, a savory ramen porridge locals fall back on because it is cheap, warm, and filling.

How to make it: Cook a cup of ramen with a little less water than the line suggests. When the noodles are almost done, open a triangle kimbap, drop the rice into the broth, and stir until the rice breaks apart and the soup thickens into a porridge. A slice of cheese or a beaten egg stirred in at the end makes it richer.

Salty, warm, and rice-heavy, it already understands a chilled bottle of Chamisul Fresh.

Jjajang Buldak is the fire-noodle fix for people who can't do full spice

One of the biggest convenience-store recipes right now mixes Buldak fire noodles with a jjajang (black-bean) ramyun like Gonghwachun. The jjajang's savory-sweet sauce coats the Buldak heat, so the bowl reads rich and smoky instead of pure fire. Any convenience-store jjajang ramyun works, which makes it the easy entry point for anyone who loves the Buldak flavor but taps out on the spice.

That balance of heat and sweetness is exactly what a chilled Chamisul Fresh is for — clean enough to reset your mouth between bites.

Cheesy chips turn the snack aisle into instant anju

Grab potato snacks (Ojamja, Pocachip, whatever is on the shelf), lay slice or string cheese on top, and microwave until the cheese melts down into the ridges. It is the lowest-effort loaded-nacho moment in Korea right now, and it films well for the cheese pull alone.

Salty, crunchy, and cheesy wants something crisp and fruity next to it — JINRO Green Grape keeps it from feeling heavy.

Cup noodle egg steam is the quiet, no-spice option

Not everything at the counter is a heat challenge. Crack an egg into a cup noodle, add a splash of water, and microwave until the egg sets into a soft, savory steamed-egg bowl. It is warm, mild, and closer to a light meal than a dare — the combo locals reach for on an easy night.

For something this gentle, keep the pour bright: JINRO Grapefruit sits next to it without taking over.

Buldak naengmyeon is the summer feed pick

First, what naengmyeon is: Korea's classic cold noodle dish — thin, chewy, springy noodles served in an icy, tangy-savory broth, the go-to way locals beat the summer heat. The viral convenience-store twist turns it spicy: Buldak fire noodles rinsed cold and tossed with chilled naengmyeon broth and ice, finished with whatever garnish makes the bowl look done. MBC's 14F covered the trend in May, and Buldak's own recipe channel lists cold Buldak noodles among the season's viral SNS recipes.

The cold-and-spicy combination is made for JINRO Plum or JINRO Grapefruit — a fruity, chilled pour that turns a hot night into a quick at-home anju table. That is the bigger rule for convenience-store anju: don't overthink it. If it is spicy, salty, quick, and shareable, it probably belongs next to JINRO.
