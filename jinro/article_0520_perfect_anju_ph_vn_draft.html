The Perfect Anju: Chicken Adobo and Lemongrass Clams

JINRO's global food series drops two new episodes — Filipino Chicken Adobo and Vietnamese nghêu hấp sả. Here are the dishes, the pairings, and the videos.

The Perfect Anju is JINRO's monthly food series on its global channels. Each episode picks one country, one dish, and pairs it with JINRO. May brings two: Manila and the Vietnamese coast.

A Quick Note on Anju

Anju (안주) is the Korean word for food eaten while drinking. It's not a side or an appetizer — it's a category of food shaped around the pace of drinking. Bold, savory, often slow to eat. The rule is simple: it should match the character of the drink. Soju is clean and slightly sweet, so anju tends to be bold, savory, and balanced with acidity or spice.

Episode 2 · Manila — Chicken Adobo

Chicken Adobo is the unofficial national dish of the Philippines. Bone-in chicken is slow-braised in soy sauce, vinegar, garlic, bay leaves, and black peppercorns, sometimes with a little sugar to balance the acidity, until the sauce reduces into something dark and glossy.

Why it works with JINRO:

  • Soy-based umami matches the same flavor profile as Korean braised dishes that traditionally pair with soju.
  • Vinegar acidity resets the palate between sips, the same role kimchi plays at a Korean table.
  • Bone-in texture is meant to be eaten slowly — the same rhythm as a long soju dinner.

Episode 3 · Vietnamese Coast — Nghêu Hấp Sả

Nghêu hấp sả is fresh clams steamed with bruised lemongrass, sliced chili, garlic, and a splash of fish sauce. Vietnamese basil sometimes goes in at the end. The broth that pools at the bottom is fragrant, salty, and clear — the dish is built around that liquid.

Why it works with JINRO:

  • Briny, ocean-clean flavor pairs naturally with soju's dry, slightly sweet finish.
  • Lemongrass aroma aligns with the same brightness JINRO already has on the nose.
  • Hand-eating with shells slows the meal down — again, the rhythm soju is built for.

Two Different Dishes, Same Pairing Logic

Chicken Adobo is warm and slow-braised. Nghêu hấp sả is light and steamed. Side by side they look unrelated — but both check the same anju boxes: bold base flavor, balancing acidity or aroma, slow-eating texture. That's the point of the series: Korean anju isn't a single type of food. It's a relationship between what's on the table and what's in the glass.
