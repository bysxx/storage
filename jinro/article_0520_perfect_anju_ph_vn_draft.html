The Perfect Anju Goes to Manila and the Vietnamese Coast

Two new episodes. Two dishes that couldn't taste more different. One bottle that makes both of them make sense. Here's the May edition of JINRO's global food series.

When we kicked off The Perfect Anju earlier this spring, the idea was simple. Korea has a word for the food you eat with a drink — anju. And we wanted to see how that idea travels.

The first episode landed in Vietnam, with gà kho sả ớt: spicy braised chicken with lemongrass. This month, the series brings two new stops. The dishes couldn't be more different from each other — one slow and dark, one bright and briny — but both belong to the same idea.

A Quick Refresher: What Is Anju?

In Korea, you don't drink without food. The food you eat alongside alcohol has its own name — anju (안주). It's not a side dish or an appetizer. It's a whole category of cooking built specifically for the pace and palate of drinking.

The rule is simple. Anju should match the character of the drink. Soju is clean, slightly sweet, and easy to sip. So it pairs naturally with food that's bold, savory, a little tangy, and meant to be eaten slowly. Sometimes that means hot and braised. Sometimes that means briny and steamed. Both fit.

Both of this month's episodes prove it — in completely different ways.

Episode 2 · Manila — Chicken Adobo

Chicken Adobo is the unofficial national dish of the Philippines. Every family has their own version. Every region has its own twist. But the foundation is always the same.

Chicken is slow-braised in soy sauce, vinegar, garlic, bay leaves, and black peppercorns. Sometimes a splash of canola oil to start the sear. Sometimes a little sugar to balance the acidity. Then it sits on low heat until the sauce reduces into something dark, glossy, and deeply savory.

The result tastes like home — salty, tangy, garlicky, with the warmth of bay leaves and the slow comfort of braised meat. Eaten with rice. Eaten with hands. Eaten over long conversations.

In other words, it tastes like anju.

Here's why it works with JINRO:

  • Bold, savory base. The soy sauce carries the same umami depth as Korean braised dishes — it stands up to soju's clean finish without getting lost.
  • Tang from vinegar. Adobo's signature acidity does the same job as kimchi at a Korean table — it cuts richness and resets the palate.
  • Slow-eating texture. Bone-in chicken is meant to be picked at slowly, which is exactly how Koreans drink soju — sip, talk, eat, sip again.
  • Layered aroma. Garlic, bay leaves, pepper, a hint of caramelized sauce. JINRO's subtle sweetness rides along without overpowering anything.

Episode 3 · Vietnamese Coast — Nghêu Hấp Sả

From Manila, the series moves to the Vietnamese coast — and the entire tempo changes.

The dish is nghêu hấp sả: fresh clams steamed with lemongrass. The recipe is almost minimalist. Clams go into the pot with bruised lemongrass stalks, sliced chili, garlic, and a splash of fish sauce. Sometimes Vietnamese basil leaves get tossed in at the end. The steam pulls the perfume of the lemongrass into the clams, and the broth at the bottom of the pot becomes its own thing — fragrant, salty, briny, the kind of liquid you want to spoon over rice or sip on its own.

You eat it with your hands. You crack open shells, dip the meat into the broth, talk between bites. The pace is unmistakably anju.

And here's where this pairing reads completely differently from Adobo:

  • Briny, ocean-clean flavor. Steamed clams carry a salinity that asks for a clean, slightly sweet drink to follow it. JINRO's crisp finish does exactly that.
  • Aromatic lift from lemongrass. The citrusy herbal note meets soju's subtle sweetness without competing with it — they share the same brightness.
  • Light heat from fresh chili. The chili is fragrant, not aggressive. It opens the palate and makes the next sip of JINRO feel cooler than it actually is.
  • Slow-eating texture. Hands, shells, broth, breaks in between — this is the rhythm soju was made for.

Two Opposite Dishes, Same Anju Logic

Chicken Adobo is dark, slow, and warm. Nghêu hấp sả is bright, fast, and briny. Side by side, they look like they belong to different conversations.

But the anju logic is identical. Both reset the palate between sips. Both reward slow eating. Both have a flavor profile clean enough to let soju do its own thing. That's the entire point of the series — Korean anju isn't a single style of food. It's a relationship between what you eat and what you drink. And once you see it, you start seeing it everywhere.

Where the Series Goes Next

Three episodes in, The Perfect Anju has covered braised chicken, soy-glazed comfort, and now steamed seafood. The point isn't to crown a single winner. It's to show that the Korean idea of anju already exists in every cuisine — sometimes hot, sometimes cool, always made for drinking with.
