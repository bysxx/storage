The Perfect Anju Isn't Just Korean — JINRO's New Series Proves It

In Korea, you never drink without food. That food is called anju — and JINRO just launched a global series to find the perfect anju in every country, starting with Vietnam.

If you've ever had soju in Korea, you know there's one unspoken rule: you don't drink alone, and you definitely don't drink without food.

The food you eat while drinking? Koreans call it anju (안주). It's not an appetizer. It's not a side dish. It's a whole category of food designed specifically to be eaten with alcohol — bold, savory, sometimes spicy, always satisfying.

Think grilled pork belly sizzling on a tabletop grill with shots of soju. Spicy braised fish stew with tofu. Fried chicken with beer. In Korea, the pairing between food and drink isn't just a preference — it's a cultural ritual.

But here's the thing. Korea doesn't have a monopoly on this idea. Every culture has dishes that were practically made to go with a drink. And that's exactly what JINRO's new series is about.

What Is Anju, Exactly?

Before we get into the series, let's talk about why anju matters.

The word anju literally breaks down to "alcohol blocker" — food that helps balance out the effects of drinking. But in practice, it's much more than that. Anju is the reason Koreans go out drinking in the first place. You don't just meet for drinks. You meet for food and drinks.

Historically, anju was served in jumaks — traditional Korean taverns — alongside makgeolli and other local brews. But as Korea's drinking culture evolved, so did the food. Today, anju ranges from simple dried snacks to elaborate hot pots and full grilled meat spreads.

The golden rule? Anju should match the strength and character of your drink. Soju, with its clean and slightly sweet profile, pairs best with bold, savory, and spicy flavors. That's why dishes like kimchi jjigae, jokbal (braised pig feet), and dakbokkeumtang (spicy braised chicken) are considered classic soju anju.

And that last one — spicy braised chicken — is exactly where this story goes next.

JINRO just launched "The Perfect Anju" — an original food series on its global social channels that takes the Korean concept of anju and applies it to cuisines around the world.

Each episode focuses on one country, one dish, and one perfect soju moment. The series is shot on location with local ingredients and real kitchens — no studio setups, no staged props. Just honest cooking, real food, and a bottle of JINRO on the table.

It's not about replacing Korean anju. It's about expanding the idea. Because if soju pairs perfectly with Korean braised chicken, why wouldn't it pair with a Vietnamese braised chicken that hits all the same flavor notes?

Episode 1: Vietnam — Gà Kho Sả Ớt

The first episode takes us to Vietnam, with a dish called gà kho sả ớt — braised chicken with lemongrass and chili.

If you know Korean dakbokkeumtang (닭볶음탕), you'll immediately see the connection. Both are braised chicken dishes. Both are savory, spicy, and deeply comforting. Both are the kind of food you want to eat slowly, with good company and something cold to drink.

But gà kho sả ớt has its own identity. The lemongrass gives it a bright, citrusy aroma that cuts through the richness of the braised chicken. The chili brings heat, but it's a different kind of heat — more fragrant, less aggressive. And the sauce is caramelized with fish sauce and sugar, giving it a sweet-savory depth that's distinctly Vietnamese.

Here's where the pairing clicks: JINRO soju's clean finish cuts right through that caramelized sauce. The slight sweetness of the soju complements the lemongrass without competing with it. And the cool, smooth sip between bites of spicy chicken? That's the exact same rhythm as eating dakbokkeumtang with soju in Seoul — just in Ho Chi Minh City instead.

Why This Pairing Works

Let's break down why gà kho sả ớt works as soju anju:

  • Bold, savory flavor — the caramelized fish sauce base stands up to soju's clean taste without being overpowered.
  • Spice with depth — the chili heat makes you reach for your glass, and soju's smooth finish cools the palate.
  • Aromatic complexity — lemongrass and shallots add layers of fragrance that complement soju's subtle sweetness.
  • Slow-eating texture — braised chicken on the bone is meant to be picked at slowly, which matches the Korean drinking pace of sipping and savoring.

In Korean drinking culture, the best anju isn't necessarily the fanciest dish. It's the one that makes you slow down, talk more, and enjoy the moment. Gà kho sả ớt does exactly that.
